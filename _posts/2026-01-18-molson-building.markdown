The half crumpled building sits at the edge of the river, just far enough away to gaze longingly at it, but close enough that if it were to stick your hands out, it could swipe the water and feel the current ripple from the interaction. Some sides are labeled “Molson,” showing the heritage of what the building, factory, once was. But now there is a “Kong” and “Sluk” added in dripping paint, showcasing to the world the power of the people.

I looked at it from a few different angles, seeing not just the graffiti most articles are written on, the ones I listed above, but also a little character and even more lettering on a different side.

I remember standing outside Radio Canada on Avenue Papineau, watching the building slowly get into view. The walk over was mostly spent sloshing through snow and leftover salt, but the building became clear at once, where you could see the emblemage in large lettering. “MOLSON.”

We walked over to get closer, traveling across a street that was more of a highway, jumping over large piles of pushed to the side snow, highlighting the fact we had been discovering all morning: this walk was not made for pedestrians.

Yes, Montréal had a lot of snow, but often they were pushed onto sidewalks while streets and bike lanes were left clear. Luckily my boots were water proof and suited for the cold, as I felt the tips of my ears red as we neared the closest side of the building by heading into an alley.

“‘Kaoe’ and ‘June?’” my expedition partner stated. We looked up at the massive building, all brick.

“We’re on the wrong side.” I replied.

However, before we could even start to move, there was music that erupted around us. It ranged from funk to rock to even some light metal. And we were stuck there, in between this building that had the renovation group fuming, and music that was meant to be alternative.

I turned around and saw a different building with windows that looked to be reconstructed after a break in. Graffiti colored the walls like it was a sketchbook, a mixture of French and English culminating together in telling everyone several swears.

We walked down the building, trying to get closer to the music and see inside, when I saw at the edge of the alley was a blocked off area, with gates held together by a lock. A railroad track was on top of it, and there was a train going by in the afternoon.

It didn’t take much to slip right through, to see right up onto the tracks. Even though the area was partly covered, snow covered the ground like litter, and I was surrounded by ice and cold.

Several objects were hidden in the snow, only brought to light when I stepped on them. These were often cups, bags, other objects that hinted towards someone staying down here in the warmer months.

However, there were also larger objects, concrete, metal, something that looked to be powered by electricity. I felt like I was in a Stalenhag novel, a character who is stuck between the past and the technology of the future. Everything felt old and rusty, but somehow so new and defined. The snow was real and tangible and cold, and brought me to reality, but the train tracks and their design made me feel like I was peaking into the last century.

I somehow felt out of place with how old everything was, but also not old enough to truly understand what I was doing there.

I slipped out shortly after, watching tags appear around me by different graffiti writers, and we continued on trying to get a better view of the Molson building, one where the other graffiti was shown.

The area was boarded up around the building, signs promising “l’heure est arrivée!” or that the time was now to redevelop. Signs warned of danger and death if entry was attempted, while others talked about the plans that had yet to come, all in French.

“Entrée Sortie Camions?” My partner asked. Eying a sign next to a green tarp, one that looked like it was moved quite frequently.

“Entrance and exit for cars.” I replied.

It was almost like we were in an old detective novel, one where the sidekick speaks the target language and tells the protagonist so they are able to continue on with the adventure. But I was the protagonist of my own life and the one that spoke French.

Eventually we walked far enough to be able to see the “Kong” and “Sluk.”

“You’d wonder how they’d get up there but now that we’re finally here you can see all the construction bits they probably got up with.” My partner stated, peering over the edge from where there was a cut in the word barricade.

“Any skinny person with a bit of climbing ability would be able to get right up that.” I commented back.

And we stood there. In the snow, next to what felt like racing cars, next to the light, next to the barrier, together. Staring at the building older than us, an old factory type, a brewery, that was going to be transformed into housing and we just stood there and looked at it.

Now I didn’t feel simultaneously stuck in the past while in the future, here I was in the now, with people loudly tagging graffiti, where social norms came into play with what was right and wrong, and the people against the corporations still raged on.

It was so strange because just fifty feet over we were surrounded by music and alternative culture, while here it was a reminder about how land owners wanted to control their property and thus image.

And here we were, two college kids staring at graffiti for a class and I must say I somehow felt so liminal. This construction site was the exact definition of an in between. It’s not a building but yet it once was.

The whole area, or “Quartier Molson” that they are proposing, is trying to modernize a venue of the past, but the residents are not letting that happen quietly. Graffiti is loud, draws attention, and is alternative. Just like that freckled window building that is a music studio across the street.

And even though my partner and I are just passing through, even though I know someday the construction will be done and someday people will forget that this building even existed, they graffiti and the moment creates a temporary permanence, even if it is just in our heads.
